The 2026 World Cup Break Guide

104 matches, 48 teams, group stage to final. When it's safe to step away, and when it isn't.

Guide · June 2026 · 2 min read

The tournament runs June 11 to July 19. Group stage ends June 26, then round of 16, quarter-finals, semis, and a final in New York. The bathroom calculation changes as the rounds progress. What's safe in a group game will cost you in a knockout.

The group stage

Dead rubbers are your best window in June. When both teams in a final group game have already secured their round-of-16 spots, or both are already out, the match loses its tension. Managers rotate, the pace drops, and the goal rate goes with it.

Early goalless stretches in evenly matched games are your next option. Victorian Plumbing studied Premier League goal timing and found only 8% of goals fall in the first 10 minutes. The opening quarter-hour is the quietest stretch in most matches.

Minutes 20 to 35, once the game has settled but neither side has broken through, tend to stay quiet. Possession is predictable, transitions are slow, neither team is panicking yet.

The knockout rounds

From the round of 16 on, dead rubbers don't exist. Both teams have everything to lose. The windows don't disappear but they shrink.

The 8% stat for early goals holds in knockout football too. Teams start cautiously when the stakes are this high. That first 10 minutes is still your safest window, right through to the final.

The second half of a tight knockout is a different thing entirely. A team trailing with 20 minutes left has no later match to protect themselves for. They push everyone forward. Victorian Plumbing's data shows 70% of late pressure and comeback goals come after the 70th minute, and in knockouts that window is worse than anything in the group stage.

Extra time is its own category. After 90 tight minutes, players are tired and defensive lines break down. Goals come in bursts rather than spread across the period. Don't go.

Penalty shootouts: don't leave. Each kick takes about 60 seconds, the crowd reacts to every one, and you'll hear it from the bathroom anyway.

Why this still isn't enough

These patterns tell you what happens on average across a lot of matches. They don't tell you what's happening in the game you're watching right now.

A "safe" minute can turn dangerous fast. A 0-0 at minute 25 between two attacking sides that both need a result is a completely different match from a 0-0 between a team that's already through and a side going through the motions. The stats can't read that. They just tell you what happened in other games at that point.
